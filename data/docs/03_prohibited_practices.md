# EU AI Act — Prohibited Practices (Article 5)

Article 5 lists AI practices considered to pose an unacceptable risk and therefore banned in the Union. The prohibitions apply from 2 February 2025, six months after the Act's entry into force. The categories below are paraphrased; the operative text is in Article 5 of the Regulation.

## (a) Subliminal, manipulative or deceptive techniques

AI systems that deploy subliminal techniques beyond a person's consciousness, or purposefully manipulative or deceptive techniques, with the objective or effect of materially distorting the behaviour of a person or group, in a manner that causes or is reasonably likely to cause significant harm.

## (b) Exploitation of vulnerabilities

AI systems that exploit vulnerabilities of a natural person or group due to age, disability, or a specific social or economic situation, with the same objective and harm criteria as (a).

## (c) Social scoring

Social scoring of natural persons by public authorities or on their behalf, leading to detrimental or unfavourable treatment that is unrelated to the contexts in which the data was originally generated, or that is unjustified or disproportionate to the social behaviour or its gravity. The prohibition was extended in negotiations to include private actors.

## (d) Predictive policing of individuals

AI systems that assess or predict the risk of a natural person committing a criminal offence based solely on profiling or assessment of personality traits and characteristics. The prohibition does not apply to AI systems used to support human assessment based on objective and verifiable facts directly linked to a criminal activity.

## (e) Untargeted scraping for facial-recognition databases

AI systems that create or expand facial-recognition databases through the untargeted scraping of facial images from the internet or CCTV footage.

## (f) Emotion inference in workplaces and educational institutions

AI systems that infer emotions of a natural person in the areas of workplace and education institutions, except where the use is intended for medical or safety reasons.

## (g) Biometric categorisation of sensitive attributes

Biometric-categorisation systems that categorise individual natural persons based on their biometric data to deduce or infer race, political opinions, trade-union membership, religious or philosophical beliefs, sex life or sexual orientation. This does not apply to lawful labelling or filtering of lawfully acquired biometric datasets in law-enforcement contexts.

## (h) Real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces

The use of real-time remote biometric identification (RBI) systems in publicly accessible spaces for the purpose of law enforcement is prohibited, except for narrowly defined situations: targeted search for specific victims of abduction, trafficking or sexual exploitation, or for missing persons; prevention of a specific, substantial and imminent threat to life or physical safety, or of a genuine and present or foreseeable terrorist attack; the localisation or identification of a person suspected of having committed a serious criminal offence listed in an annex. Any such use requires prior authorisation by a judicial authority (or, in justified urgency, ex post within 24 hours), a fundamental-rights impact assessment, and registration in the EU database. Member States may further restrict or prohibit these uses domestically.
