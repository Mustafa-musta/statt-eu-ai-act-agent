# EU AI Act — High-Risk AI Systems

High-risk AI systems are permitted to be placed on the market subject to a comprehensive set of ex-ante and ex-post obligations. Two routes lead to high-risk classification.

## Route 1 — Annex I (safety components)

AI systems intended to be used as safety components of products, or which are themselves products, covered by Union harmonisation legislation listed in Annex I, and which require a third-party conformity assessment under that legislation. This includes machinery, toys, lifts, equipment in potentially explosive atmospheres, radio equipment, pressure equipment, recreational craft, cableway installations, personal-protective equipment, gas appliances, medical devices, in-vitro diagnostic medical devices, civil aviation, two- and three-wheel vehicles, agricultural vehicles, marine equipment, rail interoperability, motor vehicles.

## Route 2 — Annex III (stand-alone systems)

Eight categories of stand-alone use cases:

1. Biometrics — biometric categorisation, emotion recognition, and remote biometric identification (where not prohibited by Article 5).
2. Critical infrastructure — safety components in the management and operation of critical digital infrastructure, road traffic, and the supply of water, gas, heating and electricity.
3. Education and vocational training — admissions, evaluation of learning outcomes, assessment of appropriate level of education, monitoring and detection of prohibited behaviour during tests.
4. Employment, workers' management and access to self-employment — recruitment and selection (CV-screening, targeted job-ad delivery), decisions affecting terms of employment, promotion or termination, task allocation based on individual behaviour or personal traits, monitoring and evaluating performance.
5. Access to essential private and public services — public-assistance benefits eligibility, creditworthiness or credit-scoring (excluding fraud detection), risk assessment and pricing for life and health insurance, dispatching of emergency services and triage.
6. Law enforcement — risk assessment of natural persons (victim or offender), polygraphs, evidence-reliability assessment, profiling in the course of detection or investigation.
7. Migration, asylum and border-control management — polygraphs, risk assessment of irregular migration or health risks, examination of applications for asylum/visa/residence-permits, detection/recognition/identification of persons except passport verification.
8. Administration of justice and democratic processes — assisting judicial authorities in researching and interpreting facts and law, applying the law; influencing the outcome of elections or referenda, or the voting behaviour of natural persons (excluding output not directly interacting with persons, such as administrative tools).

## Provider obligations

Articles 9–15 set out the substantive requirements:

- Article 9 — risk-management system established, implemented, documented and maintained throughout the lifecycle.
- Article 10 — data and data-governance: training, validation and testing datasets must be relevant, sufficiently representative, free of errors and complete to the best extent possible, with appropriate measures to detect and mitigate bias.
- Article 11 — technical documentation drawn up before placing on the market, kept up to date.
- Article 12 — automatic recording of events (logs) over the lifetime of the system.
- Article 13 — transparency and provision of information to deployers, including instructions for use.
- Article 14 — effective human oversight, including measures enabling natural persons to understand the system, monitor operation, intervene or interrupt, and override outputs.
- Article 15 — appropriate level of accuracy, robustness and cybersecurity, declared in the instructions for use.

Before placing on the market, providers must perform a conformity assessment (internal control under Annex VI for most Annex III systems, third-party for biometric systems and Annex I products), affix CE marking, register in the EU database, and put in place a quality-management system. Post-market monitoring, serious-incident reporting (within 15 days, or 72 hours for widespread infringement or critical-infrastructure disruption), and corrective actions are required throughout the lifecycle.

## Deployer obligations

Deployers of high-risk AI must use the system in accordance with the instructions, ensure human oversight is exercised by competent staff, monitor operation, keep logs for at least six months, inform workers and their representatives before deploying at the workplace, conduct a fundamental-rights impact assessment for public-sector and certain private deployments (creditworthiness, life/health insurance), and inform affected persons that they are subject to the system's output. Article 86 grants affected persons the right to obtain a clear and meaningful explanation of decisions made with the assistance of a high-risk AI system.
