# EU AI Act — Risk Classification

The Regulation takes a risk-based approach. Obligations scale with the potential harm an AI system poses. Four tiers exist for AI systems, plus a separate regime for general-purpose AI (GPAI) models.

## Unacceptable risk

Listed in Article 5. These practices are banned outright because they are deemed incompatible with EU values and fundamental rights. See the document on prohibited practices for the full list.

## High risk

Defined in Article 6 and listed in Annex III (stand-alone use cases) and Annex I (AI systems that are safety components of products covered by EU harmonisation legislation, e.g. medical devices, machinery, toys). High-risk systems are permitted but subject to extensive obligations: a risk-management system, data and data-governance requirements, technical documentation, automatic logging, transparency to deployers, human oversight, and accuracy, robustness and cybersecurity. They must undergo a conformity assessment before placement on the market and be registered in the EU database.

## Limited risk (transparency)

Article 50 imposes transparency obligations on certain AI systems regardless of risk classification. Providers of AI systems intended to interact directly with natural persons must inform users they are interacting with an AI (unless this is obvious from context). Providers of generative AI must mark synthetic content (audio, image, video, text) as machine-generated in a machine-readable format. Deployers of emotion-recognition or biometric-categorisation systems must inform exposed persons. Deployers of deepfakes must disclose that content has been artificially generated or manipulated, with exceptions for clearly artistic, satirical or fictional works.

## Minimal or no risk

The vast majority of AI systems (e.g. spam filters, recommender systems for entertainment, AI in video games) fall here. No mandatory obligations apply; the Commission and the Board encourage voluntary codes of conduct.

## Classification logic

A system listed in Annex III is presumed high-risk. However, Article 6(3) allows providers to claim a derogation if the system performs only a narrow procedural task, improves the result of a previously completed human activity, detects decision-making patterns without replacing or influencing them, or performs a preparatory task. Providers claiming the derogation must document the assessment and register the system. The Commission may adopt delegated acts to add, modify or remove use cases from Annex III.
