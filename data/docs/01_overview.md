# EU AI Act — Overview

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) is the first comprehensive horizontal regulation of artificial intelligence systems in the world. It was politically agreed in December 2023, formally adopted by the Council on 21 May 2024, signed on 13 June 2024, and published in the Official Journal of the European Union on 12 July 2024. It entered into force on 1 August 2024.

## Objectives

The Regulation pursues a dual objective: to protect fundamental rights, health, safety, democracy, the rule of law and the environment from harmful uses of AI; and to foster innovation and the uptake of trustworthy AI in the internal market. It is designed to be technology-neutral and future-proof through risk-based obligations rather than prescriptive technical rules.

## Scope

The Act applies extraterritorially. It covers:

- Providers placing AI systems on the EU market or putting them into service in the Union, regardless of where the provider is established.
- Deployers of AI systems located in the Union.
- Providers and deployers established outside the EU where the output of the AI system is used in the Union.
- Importers and distributors of AI systems.
- Product manufacturers placing AI systems on the market under their own name together with a regulated product.

Excluded from scope are: AI systems developed and used exclusively for military, defence or national-security purposes; AI used solely for scientific research and development; personal, non-professional use; and free-and-open-source AI components except where they are placed on the market as a high-risk system or as a GPAI model with systemic risk.

## Governance

Enforcement is shared between national competent authorities (typically the market-surveillance authorities of each Member State) and the new European AI Office, established within the European Commission's DG CONNECT, which is exclusively responsible for supervising general-purpose AI models. The European Artificial Intelligence Board, composed of one representative per Member State, coordinates implementation. A scientific panel of independent experts and an advisory forum provide technical input. The Act complements existing EU legislation, including the GDPR, the Digital Services Act, the Digital Markets Act, the Product Liability Directive, and sectoral product-safety regulations.
